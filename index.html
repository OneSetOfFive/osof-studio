Before OSOF

Some things start with a petition at midnight.

In June 2020, the night the George Floyd protests reached South Florida, I sat down and wrote a petition. I had lived in Plantation, Florida for years. I loved the city. But the name had always felt like a weight, and that night it became impossible to ignore. I put it on Change.org, went to sleep, and woke up to thousands of signatures.

The campaign grew fast. #PlantationNoMore and #AnythingButPlantation started circulating. By the time I stood up at city council to make my case, the petition had over 10,000 signatures. Two days later I was on CNN. Articles followed in Miami New Times, NBC 6, CBS Miami, WSVN and more. People I had never met were sharing it across the country.

The city did not change its name. But something else happened that I did not expect. I realized that one person with a clear message and the right platform could move people. That the gap between having an idea and making it heard was smaller than I thought. And I filed that away.

My Twitter handle through all of it was @OneSetOfFive. The name was already there. OSOF was already who I was. I just had not built the company yet.

The Story

I know what it feels like when the world wasn’t built for you.

“Accessibility isn’t just physical. It’s monetary. It’s knowing you have the proximity to build what you need.”

OSOF stands for One Set of Five. I was born with one hand, and I know how it feels to navigate a world designed around assumptions that don’t include you. That experience isn’t a footnote. It’s the entire foundation of why this company exists.

In December 2025 I discovered vibe coding and agentic AI, and something clicked. What followed was a deep, self-driven dive into building: apps, automations, platforms, tools, all pointed at the same problem. Small businesses and everyday people are being priced out of the technology that could change everything for them. A clinic shouldn’t have to pay $80,000 a year for software that still doesn’t fit. A small business owner shouldn’t need to pay $1,000 a month for software that isn’t even fully customized to how they actually work.

The age of personal apps and micro-SaaS is here. I built OSOF to make sure it belongs to everyone, not just the ones who could already afford a seat at the table. I’m starting small. I’m scaling with intention. And I’m just getting started.
